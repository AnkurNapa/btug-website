Five Dashboard Design Principles That Actually Stick

By Rahul Menon · 3 April 2026

A technically correct dashboard can still fail if nobody can read it. After reviewing dozens of community submissions at our viz clinics, the same handful of principles keep coming up. Here are five that consistently raise the quality bar.

1. Lead with one clear question

Every dashboard should answer a primary question above the fold. If a viewer can’t tell what the dashboard is for in five seconds, the layout needs work.

2. Establish hierarchy through scale

Make the most important number the biggest thing on the screen. Supporting detail should be visually quieter — smaller, lighter, further down.

3. Respect whitespace

Crowded dashboards feel stressful. Generous padding between elements gives the eye somewhere to rest and makes groupings obvious without heavy borders.

4. Limit your palette

Two or three intentional colours, used semantically, beat a rainbow. Reserve a bright accent for the single thing you want people to notice.

5. Design for the device

Most executives open dashboards on a phone or in a meeting projector. Test both, and don’t assume everyone has your 27-inch monitor.
